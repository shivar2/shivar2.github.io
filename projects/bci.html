Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) Project

Overview:

A Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) facilitates communication between the human brain and the external world by decoding brain signals and translating them into computer-recognizable commands. This technology holds immense potential, especially in healthcare, to assist individuals with disabilities in controlling external devices using brain activity alone. However, designing and implementing BCIs pose significant challenges, particularly in feature extraction and classification of brain signals.

Deep learning has recently become an essential tool for enhancing BCI performance, despite its reliance on large datasets. Collecting additional data from BCI users, who are often individuals with disabilities, is challenging. To address the issue of limited data, this project explored the use of synthetic EEG signal samples generated via Wasserstein Generative Adversarial Networks (WGANs).

My Contributions:

  • Researched and developed a method for classifying EEG signals related to motor imagery tasks using a convolutional neural network (CNN).
  • Employed WGANs to generate synthetic EEG data to address the problem of limited data availability.
  • Utilized transfer learning to improve the classification performance of EEG signals.
  • Tested and evaluated various preprocessing methods to enhance the quality of the EEG data used in the project.

Key Features:

  • EEG Signal Classification: Classified EEG signals from 4 motor imagery tasks (right hand, left hand, tongue, and foot) using CNNs.
  • Data Augmentation with WGANs: Generated synthetic EEG data to improve classification accuracy, overcoming data scarcity.
  • Transfer Learning: Applied transfer learning to boost the system’s performance and make it more robust.

Performance & Results:

Without the use of synthetic data and minimal preprocessing, the average classification accuracy was 71.37%. However, with the introduction of WGAN-generated data and normalization techniques, the accuracy improved to 66.34% after initial reductions due to preprocessing adjustments. This demonstrates that generating synthetic data can improve classification performance and offer solutions for future developments in BCI systems.

Applications:

  • Healthcare: Assists individuals with disabilities in controlling devices through brain signals.
  • Rehabilitation: Provides a pathway for future developments in neuro-rehabilitation and assistive technology.
